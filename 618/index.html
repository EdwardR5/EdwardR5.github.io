Distanced Learning; The New Norm

The Novel Coronavirus of 2019, or Covid for short, caused the entire world to reevaluate our system of Education. Despite the idea of a vaccine going around, there is a possibility that distanced learning may become a norm, or at least a part of a new post-covid world. With that in mind, we may have to be mindful and consider the possibility that, once Covid is done, we may still have to continue with the option of distanced learning. Therefore, it's important that we analyze and recognize some of the problems related to distanced learning. However, at the same time, we can propose some solutions to help make distanced learning more equitable for all.




Overview:
In Kumashiro's piece, he looks at how different schools around the US have been affected by the Novel Coronavirus of 2019, or Covid for short. At one point, Kumashiro discusses how "technology can enhance education, but the misuse of or over-reliance on technology can exacerbate existing resource inequalities between schools and even between homes, particularly for students in poverty, homeless students, and students with disabilities" (pg. 2). Kumashiro looks at the various problems that have arisen due to distanced learning and the challenges that come with it. Kumashiro also analyzes the negative effects of distanced learning on students across the United States.

Evaluation:
Because of how inequitable education already is, distanced learning can exacerbate that inequity. Students aren't able to fully collaborate with one another, as students would be communicating via a screen the entire time. This shift can cause more harm as students would be required to do more work outside of class, rather than within the classroom and collaborating over a project together.

Usage:
In a Computer Science curriculum, I may have a single lesson regarding the ethics of Computer Science. This would touch base on the pros and cons of the over-usage of technology. In the lesson, I would use this article to serve as an example of the various disadvantages that come with distanced learning.

Overview:
In Love's piece, Love analyzes how the Novel Coronavirus of 2019, or Covid for short, changed the way of education within the United States. Various educational institutions moved away from an emphasis on standardized testing, instead focusing on the wellbeing of students. Love explains that at the beginning of the Pandemic, in the "state of Georgia, the state's school superintendent issued a letter to teachers and parents calling for "compassion over compliance" as we all try to reach students during a pandemic. In mid-April, Atlanta's superintendent moved the district to a four-day teaching schedule. Teachers are to use Fridays for professional development and to be available to students who are behind. We need that same energy, understanding, and creativity from our school leaders when schools reopen" (pg 1). All of this to show how schools positively adapted to the change caused by Covid. At the same time, this positive change does have its negative aspects too. Love states, "I see the possibilities for an education system now forced to trust teachers, give students autonomy, and rely on the ingenuity of parents. I also see the loss of schools as places of refuge, safety, family services, and invaluable social interactions" (pg. 2). With the loss of physical school and a shift towards virtual school, the needs have changed. Love reminds the reader that even after the pandemic, we cannot go back to the pre-Covid system, and we must continue to prioritize the health and wellbeing of our students.

Evaluation:
Once schools reopen and life goes back to some normality within a post-covid world, we, as educators, must remember the benefits that came with the Covid period of Education. Some of the downsides are prevalent and important to recognize. However, as we eventually move into a Post-covid world, we have to try to merge the benefits of both pre-Covid and intra-Covid periods for Education. This merge would look like continuing to pay attention towards the wellbeing of our students, not prioritizing standardized testing, however, keeping the resources of our schools open to supply that refuge that Love talks about.

Usage:
In a Computer Science curriculum, I may have a single lesson regarding the Computer Science in Education, and how it can have heavy effects on the various ways we learn. This resource would go hand-in-hand with the Kumashiro piece. The Kumashiro piece discusses the positive and negative effects of technology, while this one discusses how technology like social media can be used to help share stories for good. In a sense, this article would showcase how the world works beyond just technology, and human interaction is more important at the end of the day.

Overview:
Delpit's piece discusses the importance of the process approach compared to simply getting answers. Delpit emphasizes that, "child-centered, whole language, and process approaches are needed in order to allow a democratic state of free, autonomous, empowered adults, and because research has shown that children learn best through those methods" (p 31). These three primary approaches – child-centered, whole language, and process approach – are key in providing stronger education for students as they help build students up and provide the space for students to feel empowered with their education.

Evaluation:
The emphasis on child-centered, whole language, process approaches are key, as it provides the space for students to be in a problem-posing classroom. In terms of a distanced learning classroom, these are even more important. Getting the answers nowadays is simple courtesy of the Internet and the vast information database out there, however, it is important to know how to get the information we need to know. Breaking down issues is incredibly important and thinking through the process to get a solution to a problem is even more important.

Usage:
Regardless of which curriculum I'm teaching, whether that's Computer Science or Mathematics, I do believe that the process is more important than simply getting the answer. With Computer Science and Programming, there are almost always multiple ways to get the same outcome. We have different methods for writing a single algorithm, some are more efficient than others – sometimes this efficiency comes at a cost of writing far more code. This resource would be used to showcase why its important to think through the process rather than jumping straight to an answer, which is incredibly important for both Computer Science and Mathematics.

Overview:
Moll, Amanti, Neff, & González formed a study looking at how households can influence and help students in classrooms. Moll, et al. were "particularly interested in how families develop social networks that interconnect them with their social environments, and how these social relationships facilitate the development and exchange of resources, including knowledge, skills, and labor, that enhance the households' ability to survive or thrive" (p 133). Their study recognized how important the lifestyle at home is towards an individual's education.

Evaluation:
The study done by Moll et al showcases how important the lifestyle at home is towards the education of an individual. With distanced learning, it's important for us to recognize that some households are more inequitable than others. The households of some students may house more problems than those of others and it's important to really understand this. As we go into a potential post-covid world, the lifestyle at home will continue to impact the education of our fellow students and peer teachers. Therefore it's important to respect the households and help when we can.

Usage:
As students come from varying households with varying perspectives and experiences, it's important for students to also understand that the lives we live at home will bleed into our educational environments. I think regardless of the curriculum, in the earlier days, I'd have my students read this as I believe it can help build that empathetic connection between students. That empathetic connection can help build the community and provide a space for students to really understand how different life is per person. This has less to do with any specific curriculum, and more to do with helping students build that empathy between one another.

Overview:
Ayers' work focuses around how important it is to value students, where we teach "for justice and care" as the title suggests. Ayers' looks at how we, as educators, can create the space for students to feel cared for and listened to. Ayers' suggests that "engagement, thoughtfulness, connectedness, valuing youngsters as third-dimensional beings with their own hopes, dreams, and capacities to build upon–these are the basics in teaching toward democracy and justice and care" (p 18). Ayers' looks at the various directions in which we can support our students, building up our students to feel valued and cared for, which ultimately help them want to pay attention and learn in our classrooms.

Evaluation:
In creating the space for our students to feel valued, we also generate the space for our students to want to go to school and learn, as they feel welcomed and, again, valued. The feeling of being important and valuable transcends education and connects with some of our basic needs, according to Maslow, of needing to be valued. In creating the space for our students, our students would want to return to our classrooms and get the most out of their education. This is important as their education will help build their character and careers. With distanced learning, we have our limitations, but we can still help foster these types of classrooms with our approach towards teaching.

Usage:
When it comes to usage, this is also important for the field of Computer Science. Our users, ultimately, are human beings. If we can make our users feel welcomed and valued, then they would generally return to our service. In one of the units of Practical Usage of Computer Science, I plan to hold a lesson touching on Psychological aspects where we look at the importance of making our users feel as though they matter – as they do.

Overview:
Nieto's discusses her experience in classrooms where culture isn't focused on. Nieto analyzes the various aspects important to effective education, such as the way one views learning. Nieto states, "how one views learning leads to dramatically different curricular decisions, pedagogical approaches, expectations of learning, relationships among students, teachers, and families, and indeed, educational outcomes" (pg 5). Nieto emphasizes the approach one takes towards education as that is one of the major influences towards effective education.

Evaluation:
How one views education serves as a major influence as some would view it as a means to learn, while others may see it as a mandatory part of life since those around them are telling them to attend school. For some, education is meaningless, for others, it may mean a lot. Striking that balance is difficult but important, but for students, understanding why they're in school is incredibly important. Nieto's piece looks from a teaching perspective, but can also be used to help students understand their own reasons for education.

Usage:
Understanding our why is more important than our what and how. In the case of Computer Science and Computer Science education, sometimes its important to remember why we started. This piece would be introduced earlier in the year and returned to throughout the curriculum to remind students why they started. That purpose can change throughout the course, but hopefully, it can help drive students to keep going with their goals. Computer Science is difficult, that is true. However, when we have a purpose or a reason to learn, it can be become less frustrating and more rewarding.
